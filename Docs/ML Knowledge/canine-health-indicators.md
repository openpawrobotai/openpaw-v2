# Canine Health Indicators — Veterinary Knowledge Base

Source: notes from a practicing veterinarian, structured into observable indicators for OpenPaw's sensing & health-analysis pipeline (#7). This is **general veterinary knowledge** and is published openly — the proprietary asset is the *dataset and trained model* (private `openpaw-ai`), not this reference.

> ⚠️ OpenPaw surfaces *early-warning signals*; it is **not** a diagnosis and does not replace a veterinarian.

## 1. Feeding habits
- Feeding behaviour is one of the most important health indicators.
- Most dogs eat enthusiastically; a sudden drop in appetite can signal underlying disease.
- Watch for **anorexia** (complete loss of appetite), nausea, and vomiting.

## 2. Activity & energy
- Healthy dogs are generally active and energetic with strong appetite and good digestion.
- A sudden stop or significant reduction in eating is often an **early** sign of illness.

## 3. Coat / fur — regular examination
- Coat condition reveals a lot about health.
- Parasitic infestations (e.g. mites) damage the coat — e.g. **mange**, which can cause **alopecia** (hair loss).

## 4. Coat quality changes
- Healthy coat: natural shine and lustre.
- Rough, dull, or unhealthy coat may indicate disease, nutritional deficiency, or parasites; affected dogs often look dull and lethargic.

## 5. Biphasic fever
- Fever → temporary recovery → fever again after a few days.
- This pattern is particularly dangerous and must not be ignored.

## 6. Mucous membranes
- Gums and conjunctiva (tissue around the eyes) should be pink to slightly reddish.
- Pale membranes often indicate an underlying problem, especially **anemia**.

## 7. Pale gums → anemia
- Anemia always reflects an underlying disease process (parasitic, viral, or bacterial).

## 8. Behavioural changes
- Sudden loss of interest in surroundings or unusual aggression can indicate serious illness.

## 9. Rabies — classic signs
- Aggression, abnormal behaviour, excessive drooling. Take very seriously.

## 10–11. Tail posture & behaviour
- Healthy: tail held up, often wagging, carried confidently.
- Tail held down/tucked or inactive for extended periods may indicate poor health or discomfort.

## 12. Abnormal repetitive behaviours (vices)
- Repeated tail-chasing/biting or self-biting other body parts.
- May be linked to nutritional or mineral deficiencies.

## 13. Primary external signs to monitor
- Feeding habits
- Coat condition
- Fever patterns
- Mucous-membrane colour
- Behavioural changes
- Drooling and aggression
- Tail posture and movement
- Repetitive self-biting behaviours
